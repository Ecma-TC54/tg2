## Notes

- The meeting is being recorded.  Our code of conduct applies to this meeting.  https://github.com/Ecma-TC54/tg2/blob/main/CODE_OF_CONDUCT.md
- Intro by Philippe, preliminary matters, proposed agenda.
- Minutes from last meeting: approved.
- See the PURL Spec Issues tab for links to popular current issues
- Summary
  - The committee discussed the ISO fasttrack process for ECMA-427 (https://ecma-tc54.github.io/ECMA-427/) and initiated planning for a second edition. Future updates will incorporate ABNF grammar and refined documentation annexes.
  - Participants evaluated using Package-URLs to identify AI models and services. The group ultimately decided to utilize property taxonomies to categorize software, hardware, and service entities.
  - The group addressed scaling concerns for high-volume traffic and explored migrating communication platforms. A decision was made to avoid implementing custom regex to maintain consistency with ECMA-262 (https://262.ecma-international.org/16.0/index.html).
- **Meeting Opening**: Philippe Ombredanne opened the TC54-TG2 meeting, reminding participants of the code of conduct available on the TC54 and package-url/purl-spec repositories. Attendees approved the previous meeting's minutes after verifying there were no objections.
- **ISO Fast track for ECMA-427**: Michael Herzog provided an update on the ISO fast track for ECMA-427, noting the process is slower than expected and the documentation is opaque. They are currently working on an explanatory document for ISO and are awaiting further instructions to complete the required templates.
- **Case-Sensitivity Issue**: Michael Herzog reported a legitimate case-sensitivity issue impacting NPM, Golang, and Ruby Gems, where the case-sensitive property should be set to true. They identified that this property is currently missing from the type definition, and John Horan will investigate this bug. Philippe Ombredanne noted that the impact is likely limited to older packages or private on-site registries.
- **Test Suite Updates**: Michael Herzog indicated that they are working on a rewrite of the test case methodology, with a key focus on creating a clear distinction between "base" and "advanced" tests. They emphasized that "base" tests are necessary for conformance, while "advanced" tests are meant to illustrate normalization activities.
- **Spec and Test Contradictions**: Michael Herzog discussed addressing contradictions between the current specification and test cases, particularly regarding the normalization of extra slashes. They noted that the committee must follow ECMA-427 for the time being but will look to refine requirements for canonical form in a second edition.
- **Documentation and Annexes**: The committee discussed the possibility of including enhanced "how to parse," "how to build," and "how to validate" documentation as official annexes to the standard in the second edition to provide better guidance.
- **ABNF Grammar Status**: Michael Herzog reported that work on the ABNF grammar is ongoing as part of the target list for the second edition. Jan Kowalleck and Daniel Scanteianu are currently awaiting feedback to finalize this component.
- **Regex Implementation**: The group discussed a request regarding regular expressions. They reaffirmed the standing decision not to implement custom, complex regex within the specification, opting instead to refer to ECMA-262 regex syntax where necessary to avoid maintainability issues.
- **New Participant Outreach**: Michael Herzog noted they have been conducting one-on-one outreach with new participants to explain the historical context of the project and understand the specific use cases of those joining the committee.
- **Second Edition Planning**: Michael Herzog, Steve Springett, and Philippe Ombredanne discussed the requirements for a second edition, including the ABNF grammar, corrections to discrepancies between the written specification and tool implementations, and the formalization of editorial documentation.
- **Version Comparison and Ecosystem Discrepancies**: The group discussed the complexities of version comparison, with Philippe Ombredanne emphasizing the need to avoid standardizing version comparison logic in the current edition due to significant differences between ecosystems like Python, Go, and Ruby.
- **Test Suite and Grammar Inconsistencies**: John Horan highlighted discrepancies between the `pytest` library analysis and the proposed ABNF grammar, noting that the test suite is intended to serve as the most detailed and accurate representation of the specification.
- **Download Statistics and Usage Visibility**: Philippe Ombredanne reported that various projects are seeing roughly 5 million downloads daily, though they acknowledged that automated downloads from continuous integration systems make it difficult to measure actual human usage accurately.
- **SID and AI-BOM Integration**: Van Lindberg introduced the need for stable identifiers for AI models and components within AI-BOMs, suggesting that PURL could serve as a useful solution for identifying hosted services and models that lack other stable identifiers.
- **Alignment with CycloneDX and AI Efforts**: Steve Springett noted that the CycloneDX team is already actively re-architecting AI representation, including "blueprints" and an extensible taxonomy of behaviors. They emphasized the importance of aligning these efforts with existing industry groups rather than duplicating work in separate AI-BOM forums.
- **Services and Hardware Identifiers**: The committee discussed the difficulty of identifying precise versions of services and hardware products. Steve Springett and Philippe Ombredanne agreed that it is currently impossible to identify specific versions of services through identifiers due to mutable environments and canary builds.
- **Private Identifiers and Localization**: Van Lindberg proposed a decentralized approach to private identifiers using reverse domains, allowing organizations to mint identifiers for internal use. They also discussed the need for qualifiers to help locate components within mono-repos, noting the difficulty of finding specific code corresponding to services.
- **PURL for IT Asset Management**: Steve Springett suggested that PURL could fill the void left by CPEs (Common Platform Enumeration), which are not widely adopted outside of the security industry, to support broader IT asset management use cases.
- **Proprietary vs. Open Source Approaches**: Michael Herzog reiterated the goal of maintaining a single PURL type to keep the system unified, even while acknowledging the differing needs of public open-source registries and proprietary supplier models.
- **SID Distributor Identity and Convergence**: Van Lindberg and Michael Herzog discussed the importance of identifiers acting as a "Schelling point," where different organizations aiming to identify the same software converge on the same PURL, rather than minting conflicting identifiers for the same item.
- **Technical Implementation of SID Locators**: Philippe Ombredanne outlined the plan for SID locators, which involves using well-known JSON files hosted on domains or Git repositories to manage name spaces and participant information. They emphasized that this approach avoids dependency on any single infrastructure provider like GitHub.
- **Scaling and Infrastructure**: Steve Springett noted that given the high volume of traffic (potentially tens of millions of requests per day), the infrastructure will need to utilize a robust CDN, such as Fastly, to handle the static registry information efficiently.
- **Dependency-Track Update**: Steve Springett reported that a bank has funded the complete rearchitecture of Dependency-Track version 5, which is designed for high scalability and is expected to be released in the next one to two weeks.
- **Service Identifiers**: Van Lindberg introduced the question of whether SID is the correct solution for representing services, proposing that PURL would be an appropriate place to include service identifiers.
- **Defining AI Services**: Philippe Ombredanne and Van Lindberg discussed the definition of a service, using platforms like Azure Foundry and AWS Bedrock as examples of aggregators over services. They noted that while a service might be an endpoint, the model identifiers are often unique to the service provider, even when the underlying upstream model remains the same.
- **PURL Taxonomy and Granularity**: Steve Springett likened PURL to a street address, suggesting that adding services simply adds more complexity to that existing structure. They recommended using a property taxonomy, similar to the model used in CycloneDX, to apply specific qualifiers based on whether the entity is software, hardware, or a service.
- **Structural Separation of IDs**: Michael Herzog expressed concern about overloading SID with too many definitions and proposed the creation of parallel structures—one for hardware, one for services, and one for software. Philippe Ombredanne and Michael Herzog discussed the potential to evolve these structures independently by assigning dedicated qualifiers appropriate to each category.
- **Deployment Models and Risk Profiles**: Steve Springett argued that the deployment model, such as whether a product is hosted or downloaded, should not dictate the identifier type. In contrast, Van Lindberg and Michael Herzog argued that these deployment methods change the legal capabilities, data retention obligations, and risk profiles, which may require distinct identifiers to accurately represent the different risk surfaces.
- **Future Collaboration and Planning**: Michael Herzog suggested moving the ongoing discussions into asynchronous threads to facilitate better commentary and stated an intention to coordinate a future meeting involving Matt Rutkowski to leverage their AI expertise. Additionally, Steve Springett and Van Lindberg agreed to connect separately to ensure their work remains compatible with CycloneDX and broader OWASP practices.
- **Communication Platform Challenges**: The participants discussed the difficulty of selecting communication platforms for open-source communities. Steve Springett highlighted concerns regarding Slack due to unexpected costs, while Philippe Ombredanne suggested that Zulip, an open-source application and service, might be a viable alternative to explore.
- The meeting was adjourned.
